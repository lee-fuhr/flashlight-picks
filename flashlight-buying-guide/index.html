How to choose the right flashlight

Updated July 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A flashlight’s output is defined by lumens (total light) and candela (beam distance), and they aren’t interchangeable, a high-lumen floodlight may not throw far. IPX8 means it passed a 1-meter submersion test but hasn’t been checked for dust, while IP68 also certifies dust-tightness. Rechargeable lights (USB-C, 18650/21750) win on daily cost and brightness, but disposable-battery models store reliably for years without charging. Your choice between a simple ‘tactical’ tail-switch light and a multi-mode EDC comes down to whether you prioritize instant, rugged operation or everyday pocket-friendly features.

Lumens vs candela: what do they actually mean?

Lumens measure the total amount of visible light coming out of the flashlight, think of it as the size of the bubble of light you create. Candela, on the other hand, measures the intensity of the beam at its center, which determines how far the light travels. A light can have high lumens but low candela (a floody wash) or modest lumens with very high candela (a pencil-thin long-range beam). Many manufacturers push lumen numbers in marketing, but if you need to see across a field or down a trail, candela matters more. For example, a 1000-lumen light with a wide reflector might only throw 150 meters, while a 500-lumen light with a deep smooth reflector can reach 300 meters. For close-up tasks like camping or reading, high lumens with broad diffusion is ideal. For search or home defense outdoors, prioritize candela. Don’t fall for the assumption that more lumens always means a better flashlight, look for both figures to match your use. Reputable brands often list both on the box or product page.

IP ratings: IPX8 vs IP68 and what they really guarantee

The IP code (Ingress Protection) has two digits: the first is for solids (dust), the second for liquids. IPX8 means the manufacturer only tested water resistance, the ‘X’ tells you dust was not evaluated, so the light could be vulnerable to sand or fine dirt. IP68 means the light passed tests for both dust-tightness (6) and continuous immersion beyond 1 meter (8). For most outdoor and EDC use, IPX8 is fine; dust ingress isn’t usually a deal-breaker unless you’re in a sandy or grimy environment. However, IP68 gives you extra confidence for rugged use like hiking in dust storms or dropping it in mud. Neither rating guarantees indefinite submersion, tests are specific depths and durations, but a properly sealed IP68 light is better protected against particle intrusion. Be wary of vague claims like “waterproof” without a code. Stick to IPX8 or IP68 from a known brand, and understand that an IPX8 light that’s also rated for a drop test is still fine for rain or accidental puddle drops.

Rechargeable vs disposable batteries: which is right for you?

Today’s rechargeable flashlights typically use standard lithium-ion cells (18650 or 21700) or built-in USB-C packs. They offer higher sustained output, recharge hundreds of times, and save money over the long run. Many high-performance lights only accept rechargeable lithium-ion, so you get more brightness and longer runtimes per charge. Disposable-battery lights, usually running on AA or CR123A. are simpler for emergency kits because alkalines and lithium primaries have a shelf life of 5 to 10 years (alkalines leak eventually; lithium disposables are better). If you need a light to work after sitting untouched in a glovebox for half a decade, disposable power is more reliable than a rechargeable that may have self-discharged or its battery aged. The honest tradeoff: rechargeable wins for daily use, cost, and performance; disposable wins for long-term emergency storage and easy replacement anywhere. Some lights accept both (e.g., an 18650 tube that will also run on two CR123As), giving you the best of both worlds.

Tactical vs EDC: the design split explained

Tactical flashlights are designed for immediate, reliable operation under stress. They usually have a rear tail switch that gives momentary-on (press-and-hold) and constant-on (click) with very few modes, sometimes just high and strobe. The body is often beefy, with a crenulated bezel. The emphasis is on simplicity and toughness, not pocket comfort. These are the lights you want for home defense or duty use. Everyday carry (EDC) lights prioritize compact size, multiple brightness levels (moonlight to turbo), and often a side switch or rotary control for mode cycling. Many include a pocket clip, magnetic tailcap, or user interfaces like ramping brightness. EDC lights are designed to disappear into a jeans pocket and serve everything from finding dropped keys to walking the dog. The key difference: tactical lights give you instant, predictable action without fumbling; EDC lights offer versatility and slimmer profiles. Neither is inherently better, the choice depends on whether you’ll ever need to use it one-handed under duress, or you just want an always-with-you tool for everyday tasks.

Beam types: flood, throw, and hybrids for real use

A floody beam produces a wide, even pool of light with minimal hotspot, ideal for close work, reading maps, or lighting up a campsite. A throwy beam concentrates light into a tight hotspot that reaches far but leaves the sides dark. Hybrid designs use a textured reflector or TIR optic to create a smooth blend: decent throw with usable spill. Most lights sold today are hybrids unless they’re explicitly marketed as “pure flood” or “ultra-thrower.” A classic example: the common “pocket thrower” style uses a small head with a deep reflector, giving 300–400 meters of range but modest spill. Pure flood lights like many headlamps or lantern-style flashlights have no reflector at all. For home use, a hybrid with a wide spill and moderate throw serves well for checking the backyard and navigating dark rooms. For hiking or security, lean toward a thrower. For bike riding or area lighting, go flood. Many modern EDC lights let you change beam pattern with optional diffusers or dual-emitter setups, but those add bulk.

How to choose based on your use case

For a **nightstand or EDC light**, look for a compact 18650 or AA-powered light with a side switch, a low moonlight mode (under 1 lumen), and a decent balance of flood and throw, around 500–800 lumens and 100–150 meters of throw is plenty. Magnetic tailcap is a nice bonus for hands-free use. For **camping**, prioritize floody beam for tent and cooking area, plus medium throw for trails. A headlamp with a 90-degree tilt is more practical than a handheld for camp chores. Battery life matters: a light that runs for hours on medium (200–400 lumens) is better than an ultra-bright that dies in 30 minutes. For **home defense**, a tactical tail-switch light that can deliver 800+ lumens momentarily is critical for identifying threats without blinding yourself in the process. A simple two-mode (high and strobe) or direct-to-high interface prevents mode confusion. Ruggedness and a pocket clip for quick draw matter more than multiple brightness levels. For a **keychain backup**, look for a true AAA or 10180 light with a single-mode twist-on design and enough output to walk in the dark (perhaps 100–200 lumens). The smallest lights sacrifice throw and runtime, but they’re better than nothing when the main light is left behind.

Frequently asked questions

How many lumens do I need for everyday use?

For most around-the-house and outdoor tasks, 300 to 800 lumens is a sweet spot, enough to light up a yard or see inside a closet without blinding yourself. A moonlight mode under 1 lumen is also very useful for reading in bed or navigating without waking others.

What does IPX8 truly mean for a flashlight?

IPX8 means the flashlight passed testing for continuous immersion in water deeper than 1 meter, typically 1.5 meters for 30 minutes, but the ‘X’ means dust ingress wasn’t tested. It’s fine for rain, drops in puddles, or even short submersion, but it may not be fully dust-sealed.

Can I use rechargeable 18650 batteries in any flashlight?

No. Only flashlights specifically designed for unprotected or protected 18650 cells can safely use them. Attempting to use a lithium-ion cell in a light made for AA or CR123A batteries can cause overheating or damage. Always check the manufacturer’s battery compatibility list.

What is the strobe function for?

Strobe is a fast flashing mode often found in tactical lights intended to disorient an attacker temporarily. It can also be used as a distress signal. It’s not useful for everyday tasks and consumes battery quickly, but some users like having it for self-defense backup.

Is a tactical flashlight better than an EDC for home defense?

Generally yes, because tactical lights have a simpler interface (single tail button) that gives momentary-on without fumbling for a side switch. They’re also built more ruggedly and often have a strike bezel. But many EDC lights with a dedicated mode memory and a forward-clicky tail switch can also work well for defense.

Should I choose a light with built-in charging or removable batteries?

Built-in USB-C charging is convenient for daily use, one cable charges everything. Removable batteries let you swap a fresh cell immediately and keep spare batteries for long trips. If you prioritize reliability and extended trips, go with removable. If simple daily charging is key, built-in is fine.

In shortThe best flashlight is the one that matches your actual needs, not the one with the biggest number on the box. Start by defining your main use, daily pocket carry, camping, home defense, or emergency backup, then prioritize lumens and candela accordingly, choose a battery system that fits your lifestyle, and don’t overpay for IP68 if IPX8 is enough. A well-chosen flashlight from a reputable brand should last you years with minimal fuss.
