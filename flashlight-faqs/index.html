Flashlight questions answered

Updated July 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Flashlight specs can be confusing, but the key is to match the light to your task: lumens tell you total brightness, while candela tells you how far it throws; water ratings like IPX4 are fine for rain, but IP68 handles submersion; rechargeable batteries save money for daily use, but disposables store better for emergencies; tactical lights prioritize momentary switches and strike bezels, while EDC lights favor compact size and smooth beams; and the turbo mode drains a battery in minutes because the high current generates extreme heat and inefficiency, whereas low mode sips power for hours.

Lumens vs candela: what's the difference?

Lumens measure the total amount of light coming out of the flashlight, think of it as the flood of brightness in your immediate area. A high-lumen number means a lot of light, but it might scatter widely or stay close. That’s where candela comes in. Candela measures the intensity of the beam’s hotspot, how tightly focused that light is. A flashlight with high candela (often labeled in thousands or tens of thousands) throws a narrow, piercing beam that reaches far down a trail or across a field, even if its lumen count is moderate. For close-up tasks like reading a map, you want more lumens and less candela. For spotting something at a distance, you want higher candela even if total lumens are lower.

IPX vs IP68 water ratings: what do they mean?

IPX ratings (IPX4, IPX7, IPX8) are a simple way to judge water resistance without getting into arcane standards. The ‘X’ means no dust protection tested; the number is the water protection level. IPX4 handles splashes from any direction, fine for rain or a wet counter. IPX7 survives immersion in up to one meter of freshwater for thirty minutes, which covers accidental drops in a puddle or toilet. IP68 is the highest common rating for flashlights and means the light can be submerged deeper than one meter (often specified by the manufacturer, such as two meters) for a longer period, usually thirty minutes or more. Unless you plan to dive with your flashlight, IPX7 is plenty rugged for most outdoor and emergency use. Remember that rubber seals degrade over time, so no water rating is permanent, check the O-rings occasionally.

Rechargeable vs disposable batteries: which is better for daily carry vs emergency storage?

For everyday use, rechargeable batteries, like 18650 lithium-ions or common NiMH AA/AAA. are cheaper over the long run and more convenient because you recharge them at night rather than buying spares. They also hold a consistent voltage until nearly empty, so the beam stays bright longer without dimming. For emergency kits or prepping, disposable lithium primary batteries (not alkalines) are ideal. They have a shelf life of ten to fifteen years and resist leaking, unlike alkaline cells that can corrode inside a flashlight if left for months. Rechargeables self-discharge even when unused, so they need occasional topping off. A good compromise is keeping a flashlight with rechargeable batteries for daily carry and a separate backup with lithium disposables for the glove box or bug-out bag.

Tactical vs EDC flashlight: what's the difference in design priorities?

Tactical flashlights are built around self-defense and professional use. They typically have a tail-cap switch that allows momentary-on (press for light, release for off), a crenelated strike bezel that can be used as a last-ditch impact tool, and a very focused beam for blinding an attacker. They tend to be larger, with aggressive knurling for grip, and often multiple brightness modes selectable via the tail switch. EDC (everyday carry) flashlights prioritize compactness, ease of pocket carry, and a beam profile that works well for both close-up and moderate distance. They usually have side switches, smooth bodies to avoid snagging, and a more floody beam. If you need a light primarily for walking the dog, fixing things under the hood, or finding something dropped in the dark, an EDC light is more practical. If you work security, carry a firearm, or want a dedicated defensive tool, a tactical light makes sense.

Why is the brightest mode runtime so much shorter than low-mode runtime?

It comes down to physics and the LED’s efficiency curve. A flashlight on its highest mode (often called turbo or high) pushes the battery to deliver maximum current, which generates heat and electrical resistance. As the LED gets hotter, its efficiency drops, a phenomenon called thermal rollback. The circuit may even automatically reduce power to prevent damage, so the advertised runtime on high is often just a few minutes before it starts stepping down. On low mode, the current is tiny, heat is negligible, and the LED operates in its most efficient region. The same battery that lasts twenty minutes on turbo can easily run for fifty or a hundred hours on low. That’s why manufacturers list two wildly different runtime figures: one for a ‘brightness ceiling’ burst and one for sustainable use. Always pay attention to the low-mode runtime if you need the light to last through an emergency.

Frequently asked questions

How many lumens do I need for everyday use?

For tasks like walking the dog, rummaging in a bag, or finding a keyhole, 100 to 300 lumens is plenty. A 300-lumen light with a good beam pattern will feel very bright indoors and adequate outdoors. For trail running or checking a large dark yard, consider 500 to 1000 lumens.

What does IP68 actually guarantee?

IP68 means the flashlight can be submerged in water deeper than 1 meter, usually 1.5 or 2 meters, for at least 30 minutes. But the manufacturer must specify the exact depth and duration, so check the fine print. It’s a strong rating but not a license to use the light as a diving torch unless it’s explicitly rated for that.

Can I use rechargeable batteries in a flashlight designed for disposable cells?

Generally yes, but with caution. If the flashlight was designed for alkaline AAs or AAAs, you can often replace them with NiMH rechargeables (same voltage, around 1.2V instead of 1.5V). Some lights may be slightly dimmer, but they’ll work fine. However, do not put 3.7V lithium-ion cells (like 18650s) into a flashlight built only for 1.5V disposables, the higher voltage can fry the electronics or cause catastrophic failure. Check the manual for battery compatibility.

What is candela and why should I care?

Candela tells you how intense the center of the beam is, how far it throws light. A flashlight with 10,000 candela will light up objects farther away than one with 2,000 candela, even if both have the same lumen output. If you need to see clearly across a field or down a hallway, look for higher candela numbers. For walking around indoors, moderate candela is fine.

Why does my flashlight get hot on turbo mode?

Turbo mode pushes the LED far beyond its normal operating current to produce maximum light. That excess energy turns into heat faster than the flashlight can dissipate it. Most quality lights will automatically step down the brightness after a minute or two to protect the LED and the battery from thermal damage. This is normal and prevents the light from frying itself.

Should I buy a tactical flashlight if I don't need self-defense features?

Probably not. Tactical lights are heavier, bulkier, and have a very focused beam that can be harsh for close-up tasks. The momentary tail switch can be awkward for casual use. A good EDC light with a pocket clip, side switch, and a floody beam will serve you better for everyday tasks. Reserve tactical lights for when you specifically need a defensive tool or duty-grade light.

In shortNo single flashlight does everything perfectly. Choose a light that matches how you’ll actually use it: high candela for distance viewing, high lumens for area lighting, IPX7 or better for wet conditions, rechargeable batteries for daily use, and disposables for long-term storage. Prioritize a beam pattern and form factor that feels natural in your hand, and remember that turbo mode is a short burst, not a steady state. The right flashlight is one you’ll actually carry and enjoy using.
